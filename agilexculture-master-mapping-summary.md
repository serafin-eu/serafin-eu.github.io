# Agile × Culture — Master Mapping Summary

> A mapping of core Agile principles against three cultural intelligence frameworks:
> **Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions**, **Trompenaars' 7 Dimensions**, and **Meyer's Culture Map**.

---

## 1. Self-Organizing Teams

**Hofstede — Power Distance (PDI):** High PDI cultures (Malaysia, Mexico) expect managers to assign tasks. Flat team autonomy feels like abandonment or chaos.

**Trompenaars — Achievement vs. Ascription:** In ascription cultures, authority is granted by title and seniority, not competence. A junior developer leading a sprint is culturally illegitimate.

**Meyer — Hierarchical vs. Egalitarian:** In hierarchical cultures (Japan, Korea), team members wait for explicit direction. The Agile tenet that "the best architecture emerges from self-organized teams" is a foreign concept.

⚠️ *High friction in 60%+ of global cultures.*

---

## 2. Transparency & Daily Standups

**Hofstede — Individualism (IDV):** Collectivist cultures (China, Japan) avoid public admission of blockers — "losing face" for the individual reflects poorly on the entire group.

**Trompenaars — Neutral vs. Affective:** Neutral cultures (Japan, UK) suppress emotional signals, making standups performative. Affective cultures (Brazil, Italy) may derail them with emotion.

**Meyer — Low-context vs. High-context:** High-context communicators (Saudi Arabia, China) consider explicit public status reporting crude. Problems are communicated indirectly or not at all.

⚡ *Context-dependent — ceremony redesign needed.*

---

## 3. Iterative Delivery & Sprints

**Hofstede — Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI):** High UAI cultures (Greece, Japan, Belgium) are deeply uncomfortable with ambiguity. Delivering "incomplete" software feels irresponsible — they want full specs before starting.

**Trompenaars — Sequential vs. Synchronic Time:** Sequential cultures (Germany, Switzerland) treat sprint timelines as contracts. Synchronic cultures (Latin America, Middle East) overlap activities and resist fixed time-boxes.

**Meyer — Linear vs. Flexible Time:** Flexible-time cultures accept interruptions and task-switching mid-sprint. Sprint boundaries are treated as suggestions, not commitments.

⚠️ *Structural conflict with sprint cadence.*

---

## 4. Retrospectives & Open Feedback

**Hofstede — Individualism & Masculinity (MAS):** High MAS cultures (Japan, Austria) treat failure as individual weakness. Retros become blame sessions or are met with silence.

**Trompenaars — Specific vs. Diffuse:** Diffuse cultures (China, Russia) don't separate professional feedback from personal identity. Criticising a process feels like criticising the person.

**Meyer — Direct vs. Indirect Negative Feedback:** The single most impactful Meyer dimension for Agile. Indirect-feedback cultures (Japan, Thailand) will never say "this sprint failed" — the facilitator must read signals others would miss.

⚠️ *Highest risk ceremony — redesign critical.*

---

## 5. Customer Collaboration Over Contracts

**Hofstede — Long-term Orientation (LTO):** Short-term oriented cultures (USA, UK) want defined deliverables. Long-term cultures (China, Japan) are comfortable with evolving relationships and organic outcomes.

**Trompenaars — Universalism vs. Particularism:** Universalist cultures (USA, Germany) demand contractual clarity even in Agile. Particularist cultures (China, Russia) prioritize relationship over rules — naturally Agile in spirit, but not in process.

**Meyer — Task-based vs. Relationship-based Trust:** Relationship-based cultures (Brazil, China) need months of trust-building before productive collaboration. Agile's "start fast, build together" model skips this entirely.

⚡ *Relationship investment upfront pays dividends.*

---

## 6. Responding to Change

**Hofstede — Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI):** High UAI cultures view mid-project change as a sign of poor planning or incompetence — not responsiveness. Change requests trigger anxiety, not agility.

**Trompenaars — Inner-directed vs. Outer-directed:** Outer-directed cultures adapt naturally to environmental signals. Inner-directed cultures follow internal plans and resist external pressure to pivot.

**Meyer — Deciding: Consensual vs. Top-down:** Consensual cultures (Scandinavia, Netherlands) take longer to approve changes but implement faster. Top-down cultures (France, China) change direction quickly from the top but face bottom-up resistance.

✅ *Adaptable — depends on the origin of the change.*

---

## 7. Working Software Over Documentation

**Hofstede — Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI):** High UAI cultures (Germany, Japan) rely on comprehensive documentation to reduce risk. "Just ship it" creates existential discomfort.

**Trompenaars — Universalism vs. Particularism:** Universalists need process documentation as a universal rule-set. Removing it feels like removing accountability.

**Meyer — Principles-first vs. Applications-first:** Principles-first cultures (France, Germany) need theoretical grounding before adopting practices. "Why do we skip full documentation?" requires a philosophical answer, not just a pragmatic one.

⚡ *Lighten — don't eliminate documentation.*

---

## 8. Individuals & Interactions

**Hofstede — Individualism vs. Collectivism (IDV):** Collectivist cultures (most of Asia, Latin America, Africa) prize group harmony over individual expression. The "individuals" in Agile reads as Western individualism that can fracture team cohesion.

**Trompenaars — Communitarianism vs. Individualism:** Communitarian cultures align decisions to group consensus. Individual velocity metrics and personal story ownership conflict with shared collective responsibility.

**Meyer — Confrontational vs. Avoids Confrontation:** Confrontation-avoiding cultures (Japan, Indonesia, Thailand) will never surface a disagreement in a team meeting — even critical blockers stay invisible.

⚠️ *Reframe as "Team interactions" rather than individual.*

---

## Impact Summary

| Agile Principle | Overall Cultural Risk | Key Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| Self-organizing teams | 🔴 High | Introduce flat structure gradually; formalize SM role |
| Transparency & standups | 🟡 Medium | Redesign ceremony for high-context cultures |
| Iterative delivery & sprints | 🔴 High | Invest in story clarity; manage UAI anxiety |
| Retrospectives & feedback | 🔴 High | Use anonymous tools; reframe as system improvement |
| Customer collaboration | 🟡 Medium | Front-load relationship and trust investment |
| Responding to change | 🟢 Low–Medium | Clarify change origin; match decision style |
| Working software over docs | 🟡 Medium | Keep lightweight documentation for high-UAI teams |
| Individuals & interactions | 🔴 High | Shift language from "individual" to "team" |
